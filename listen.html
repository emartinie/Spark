A Note on Trust, Hanukkah, and What I Believe

Chanukah did not begin as a legend about miracles. It began as a response to loss. In the second century BCE, the Temple in Jerusalem had been desecrated. Practices that once oriented daily life toward God were interrupted, forbidden, or distorted. When the Temple was finally reclaimed, those who returned to it faced a simple but profound question: What does it mean to dedicate something again after it has been violated? The answer was not triumphal. It was practical and reverent. The space was cleaned. Worship resumed. A decision was made to mark the moment — not because everything was fixed, but because it mattered to begin again. Chanukah, at its root, is the memory of that choice. A decision to restore meaning where it had been disrupted. A willingness to tend what is sacred, even when the future is still unclear.

— How the Story of Light Deepened

Centuries later, the world that gave birth to Chanukah changed again. The Temple was no longer standing. The rituals tied to it could no longer be practiced as they once were. What had been dedicated could no longer be physically restored. In that context, Jewish teachers began to tell the story of Chanukah differently — not to replace history, but to carry its meaning forward. A tradition emerged that spoke of a small amount of consecrated oil, enough for one day, lasting for eight.

Whether understood as history, symbol, or sacred imagination, the story did important work. It shifted the focus from military success to spiritual endurance. From victory achieved by force to light sustained by faithfulness. For a people living without a Temple, the message mattered: holiness could still be tended. light could still be guarded. dedication did not end with loss. In this way, the story of oil did not erase the meaning of Chanukah — it protected it.

— A World Shaped by Light and Time

To understand Jesus (and Jews), it helps to remember that His world was not shaped primarily by abstract ideas, but by lived rhythms. Meaning was carried through time — through feasts, seasons, stories, and practices that returned year after year. Light, in particular, already spoke a familiar language. It marked sacred space. It signaled dedication. It stood for God’s nearness in moments when certainty was scarce. First-century Judaism held memory carefully. History was not merely recorded; it was inhabited. Feasts did not simply recall past events — they allowed each generation to step back into the same questions again: Who are we? What do we dedicate ourselves to? How do we remain faithful when the world changes? This way of living mattered because it kept faith from becoming detached. Belief was not separated from time, body, or community. It was practiced. Remembered. Re-entered. Light was not an idea to be agreed with, but something to be.

In such a world, words spoken during a feast carried more than information. They carried resonance. They echoed layers of memory and expectation already present in the hearts of those listening. — Imagining the Unrecorded Hours

The Gospels do not tell us everything Jesus did. They never intended to. There are moments where He disappears from the narrative — not because nothing happened, but because not everything needed to be said. One such moment appears during the Feast of Dedication, when Jesus is recorded as being in the Temple precincts, walking, teaching, present — yet not fully accounted for. To imagine those hours is not to invent a story, but to step into what we know of that world. The Temple during Chanukah would not have been silent. Lamps burned. Psalms were remembered. People moved slowly through familiar spaces, carrying with them generations of memory — dedication after desecration, worship restored after interruption. Jesus, as a Jewish man shaped by these rhythms, would not have been a stranger to this atmosphere. He had grown up with these stories. He had walked through seasons of light and loss His entire life. To be present in the Temple during the Feast of Dedication was not incidental — it was participatory. One can imagine Him watching the lamps. Listening more than speaking. Standing among those who knew what it meant to tend light when certainty was thin. Perhaps He prayed. Perhaps He observed the people. Perhaps He allowed the meaning of the moment to speak before He did.

First-century Judaism made room for such quiet faithfulness. Not every holy act was dramatic. Much of devotion happened in attentiveness — in being present to God’s nearness without needing to name it immediately. When Jesus later spoke of Himself as light, those words did not fall into a vacuum. They emerged from a shared world already asking the same questions: How does light endure? What does faithfulness look like when the future is unclear? Who truly belongs to our God? The Gospels record the words and they leave us room to imagine the silence that preceded them.

— Standing in a Shared Space

The Temple was one of the few places where differences did not need to be resolved in order to coexist and it will be again one day. People stood there carrying very different expectations, questions, and hopes, yet they shared the same ground, the same rhythms, the same light. Some came confident in their understanding. Some came burdened by doubt. Some came simply because this was where their people had always come while some came to get closer to the Creator or receive forgiveness through offering.

First-century Jewish life made room for this variety although there were exceptions. Unity did not require uniformity. Belonging did not depend on agreement unlike in most churches. What mattered was presence — showing up within the shared story, even when one’s place in it felt uncertain. Jesus stood in that space without forcing resolution. He did not demand that everyone see what He saw all at once. He did not divide the crowd into those who understood and those who did not. Yet He remained among them — walking, listening, present. This, too, was a kind of light: not illumination that overwhelms, but light that allows people to see as they are able. In a world shaped by prophesied scattering and return, by loss and remembrance, such presence mattered and still does. It made room for encounter without erasure, for meaning without coercion. And as I often say, "people don't care how much you know until they know how much you care."

— A Note from the Record

One brief detail preserved in the New Testament places Jesus in Jerusalem during the Feast of Dedication, walking in the Temple precincts. The text does not explain why this moment matters. It does not pause to interpret it. It simply situates Him there — among the people, in the season, sharing the same rhythms of memory and light. For readers who follow the New Testament, this detail may carry additional meaning. For others, it stands as a historical note: one more indication that the world of Chanukah was a lived, shared reality, inhabited by many voices and experiences. Either way, the feast itself remains the center of that brief part of the story. It's no small matter that no matter how you translate it, Jesus was participating in it. — How Chanukah Continued

By the fourth century, Christian communities had begun to organize their calendar differently, emphasizing other moments and meanings and I won't go into it because that just causes arguments. Distinct traditions took shape, each following its own rhythms and theological emphases. Yet Chanukah remained what it had always been: a Jewish remembrance of dedication. A practice rooted in history, sustained by meaning rather than mandate and not made up or merged (other than maybe the oil story). It did not need to compete. It did not need to explain itself. It endured because it continued to offer light without requiring agreement. In this way, Chanukah quietly resisted disappearance. It lived not in institutions alone, but in kitchens, windows, courtyards, and hearts — wherever people chose to keep tending what mattered in life.

— What Chanukah Offers to Those Looking Inward

For many Jewish readers, much of this may already feel familiar — not as information, but as lived inheritance. Chanukah has long been understood not merely as a story, but as a practice shaped by memory, family, and return. For others, especially Christians, Chanukah may feel less familiar although prophetically you are hearing about these things more and more and that will increase.

It is often encountered at a distance on a form of media — as a seasonal parallel, a cultural curiosity, or something quietly set beside other holidays without much explanation. Yet when approached on its own terms, Chanukah offers something that many Christians sense they are missing, even if they cannot yet name it. It offers a way of holding faith that is not rushed toward resolution. A way of honoring history without freezing it. A way of practicing devotion without demanding certainty. Christians can celebrate light and re-dedication however they want or they can follow the lead of their older brother and celebrate together.

Chanukah does not ask its observers to solve theological questions. It asks them to tend light. To remember dedication. To show up again the next night, and the next, not because the outcome is guaranteed, but because the act itself matters. For Christians shaped primarily by conclusions — by creeds, statements, and declarations — this can feel quietly different, even alien. Chanukah speaks in verbs rather than propositions. It teaches by repetition rather than explanation. Meaning accumulates not through argument, but through presence. This does not diminish faith. It deepens it. And for Jewish readers, this difference may be precisely the point: Chanukah was never meant to convince anyone of anything. It was meant to preserve a people’s way of remembering similar to Passover and even other Biblical feasts— a way that could survive loss, exile, and time itself. In this sense, Chanukah becomes a meeting place. Not because everyone arrives with the same understanding, but because everyone arrives with hands empty enough to receive light.

— A Feast Remembered, A Feast Shared

Chanukah occupies a unique place in Jewish life. It is not a pilgrimage festival commanded in the Torah, nor a holy convocation requiring the Temple. It is, instead, a national remembrance — closer in spirit to a day like Independence Day than to Yom Kippur or Passover. It remembers a people’s survival, resilience, and rededication after desecration. It marks a moment in history when identity was preserved, not through dominance, but through faithfulness. In this way, Chanukah belongs unmistakably to the Jewish story, not the northern House of Israel. And yet, like many national remembrances, it has always carried an open quality. Just as a nation welcomes those who join it to celebrate its history and values, Chanukah has never been guarded as an exclusive ritual necessarily. Those who find themselves drawn toward the God of Israel have long been encouraged to participate — to light, to remember, to stand alongside — not as replacements, but as companions as the Bible calls them.

This distinction matters. Participation is not the same as obligation. In the biblical vision, there are festivals that remain particular, and festivals that widen outward. The prophets speak of a time when all nations will come to Jerusalem to observe Sukkot — the Feast of Tabernacles — a festival centered on dwelling, provision, and God’s presence among humanity. That feast, not Chanukah, is described as universal in its future observance.

Chanukah, by contrast, remains what it has always been: a Jewish remembrance, generously shared. A national story, hospitable rather than closed. A light others may tend alongside Israel, without needing to claim it as their own, and I am not talking about the nation-state. This is not hierarchy. It is harmony — different rhythms held together without confusion. For those who feel scattered — culturally, spiritually, or historically — Chanukah offers something rare. It allows participation without possession. It invites remembrance without erasure. It makes space for people to stand near a story, receive its light, and honor its meaning, even if it is not theirs by birth. And perhaps this is part of its quiet gift to the world: a way of sharing light without collapsing difference, a way of belonging without ownership, a way of drawing near without taking over.

— A Note on Trust, Hanukkah, and What I Believe:

I want to be transparent about what I believe and why I’m sharing it openly. As a Christian, I haven’t abandoned my faith; I’ve just learned how to hold it more honestly, with greater respect for history and context. In particular, when we talk about Jesus, I believe it matters to acknowledge the Jew He was, the Jewish world he was born into and the historical timing surrounding events like Hanukkah, rather than ignoring or flattening them. For my Christian friends, this is an invitation to learn more deeply if you wish. I can help. And for my Jewish friends, this is simply openness — no hidden agenda, no bait-and-switch. I don’t mind saying plainly what I believe, and I also don’t mind being understood for what I do and do not claim. Trust matters more to me than pretending otherwise.
